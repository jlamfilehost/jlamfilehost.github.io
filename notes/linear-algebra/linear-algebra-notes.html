# Intro to Linear Algebra ### Prof. Smyth ### website: faculty.cooper.edu/smyth ### textbook: Elementary Linear Algebra, 10th Edition (Howard Anton) --- ### Basic vector definitions **vector**: - informal: a quantity with a magnitude and a direction - formal: any element of a vector space Examples: force, velocity, acceleration, displacement For this course, we deal mostly with 2D and 3D vectors, although we will also work with arbitrary nD vectors. **scalar**: a quantity with magnitude only A 2D or 3D vector may be represented by an arrow with length corresponding to the vector's magnitude, direction same as that of the vector. - Note that arrow has a starting and ending position, which do not exist in a vector **standard position**: when the tail of a vector lies at the origin --- ### Adding vectors (geometrically) Only vectors from the same space (dimensionality) can be added. **tip-to-tail addition**: geometric version of vector addition, put head of one of first addend to the tail of the second; the vector sum has the same tail as the first addend and the same head as the second - Using tip-to-tail diagram and reversing the order of addends makes it easy to see that the two are commutative; i.e., the resulting diagram forms a parallelogram, which meet at the tail and the head of the vector sum **zero vector**: a vector with length 0 and no direction - Note that this is not the same thing as 0 (the scalar value) $\vec{0}+\vec{v}=\vec{v}+\vec{0}=\vec{v}$ $-\vec{v}$: a vector with same magnitude as $\vec{v}$ but opposite direction $-\vec{v}+\vec{v}=\vec{0}$ --- ### Subtracting vectors (geometrically) Define: $\vec{v}-\vec{w}=\vec{v}+(-\vec{w})$ Note that $\vec{w}+(\vec{v}-\vec{w})=\vec{v}$, i.e., the difference is what you add to $\vec{w}$ to get $\vec{v}$. If both vectors are given with the same starting point, then the difference is the vector whose tail is at the head of $\vec{w}$ and whose head is at the head of $\vec{v}$. --- ### Scaling vectors (geometrically) Let k be the scale factor. - Case 1: $k>0$, $\vec{v}\neq\vec{0}$ $k\vec{v}$ is vector whose magnitude is k times the magnitude of $\vec{v}$ and whose direction is the same as the direction of $\vec{v}$. - Case 2: $k<0$, $\vec{v}\neq\vec{0}$ $k\vec{v}$ is vector whose magnitude is k times the magnitude of $\vec{v}$ and whose direction is the opposite as the direction of $\vec{v}$. - Case 3: $k=0$ or $\vec{v}=\vec{0}$ $k\vec{v}=\vec{0}$
